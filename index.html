Our philosophy

We started Keystone Acoustics because the keyboards we wanted didn't exist.

Mass production optimizes for speed and margin. We optimize for feel.

That means sourcing switches by hand, testing dozens of foam and plate combinations for a single model, and rejecting builds that don't meet our sound profile, even after hours of work.

A Keystone board is judged the same way a luthier judges a guitar: by how it responds under the hand, and how it sounds when it does.

Craftsmanship

Built the slow way, on purpose.

Machined, not molded

No seams. No flex. No compromise.

Our cases start as solid blocks of aluminum or brass. Each one is CNC-machined to tolerances of 0.02mm, then hand-finished with a bead-blasted or anodized surface. No compromise between how a board looks and how it holds up.

Tuned for sound

A sound that feels intentional.

We layer foam, film, and gasket mounts to shape a specific acoustic signature: deep and rounded, or crisp and articulate, depending on the build. Every switch is lubed by hand, one at a time, under magnification.

Stabilizers that disappear

Controlled, from the smallest key to the largest.

Rattle and inconsistency ruin an otherwise excellent board. We clip, band, and lube every stabilizer individually before final assembly, so the largest keys feel as controlled as the smallest.

Finished by hand

Anything short of perfect doesn't ship.

Keycaps are dye-sublimated or double-shot molded to our specifications, then inspected under bright light for legend clarity and color consistency before a single unit leaves the bench.

The collection

The process

From conversation to keystroke.

01

Consultation

We start with a conversation about how you type, what you value in sound and feel, and where the board will live.

02

Design

You choose case material, layout, switch profile, and finish. We advise where it helps and stay quiet where it doesn't.

03

Build

Your board is assembled, tuned, and tested by a single craftsperson from start to finish.

04

Delivery

Each unit ships in a fitted case with a written record of its specifications and the name of the person who built it.
